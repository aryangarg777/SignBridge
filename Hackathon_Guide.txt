SIGNBRIDGE: A BEGINNER'S GUIDE FOR HACKATHON JUDGES & TEAMMATES
===============================================================

Hi Team! This document explains exactly how our project, "SignBridge", works from start to finish. If a judge asks you, "How did you build it?", or "What does this file do?", use this guide to answer easily.

Think of SignBridge like Zoom mixed with real-time AI translation for deaf and hard-of-hearing individuals. 

---

PART 1: THE BIG PICTURE
How does the app actually work when someone opens it?

1) User Opens the Web App
   - The browser asks for camera and microphone permissions.
   - Once approved, Google's "MediaPipe" (an AI vision library) immediately looks at the camera feed and draws a 3D skeleton over the user's hands.

2) The AI Scans the Hands
   - The frontend browser counts the locations (X, Y, Z coordinates) of 21 knuckles per hand.
   - Since there are 2 hands, 21 knuckles, and 3 directions (X,Y,Z), that equals exactly 126 numbers. 
   - The browser packages these 126 numbers and fires them to our backend Python server almost instantly.

3) The Server Translates
   - Our Python server (Flask) takes those 126 numbers and runs them through our pre-trained Machine Learning brain (a RandomForest Classifier).
   - The brain says, "Ah! Those 126 numbers look identical to the sign language gesture for 'HELP'". 
   - It sends the word "HELP" back to the web browser.

4) Video Calling (WebRTC)
   - While the AI translation is happening locally on your screen, we are also using a technology called "WebRTC". 
   - WebRTC lets two browsers connect directly to each other (peer-to-peer) so they can stream video and audio instantly, just like FaceTime!

PART 2: WHAT DOES EACH FILE DO?

It's very important to know which code does what. Here is our folder structure:

# THE BACKEND (The Brains / Python)

* app.py
  - What it is: The central command post. 
  - What it does: It keeps the server running. When the browser sends those 126 hand coordinates here, `app.py` passes them to the AI model, figures out what word was signed, and sends the text back. It also manages the "Room Codes" when two people want to start a video call.

* train.py
  - What it is: The teacher for our AI.
  - What it does: We run this file BEFORE the hackathon. It reads thousands of rows of old hand data from our spreadsheet (`gesture_data.csv`), learns the patterns (e.g., "this pattern means 'A', that pattern means 'B'"), and saves that knowledge into files ending in `.pkl`.

* gesture_data.csv
  - What it is: Our textbook for the AI.
  - What it does: A massive spreadsheet holding recorded X, Y, Z locations of hands, and what letter/word they correspond to.

* gesture_model.pkl (and other .pkl files)
  - What it is: The "Brain" file.
  - What it does: After `train.py` finishes teaching, it saves the final AI brain into these `.pkl` files. `app.py` loads these brains when the server turns on so it can translate fast.


# THE FRONTEND (The Face / Web Pages)

* templates/index.html
  - What it is: The skeleton of our web page.
  - What it does: It creates the boxes, buttons, text fields, and areas where the Local and Remote video feeds sit. 

* static/style.css
  - What it is: The "Makeup".
  - What it does: This makes the app look like a multi-million dollar SaaS product. It makes the buttons glow, the background animate, and the floating glass panels look blurry and premium (called "Glassmorphism"). Judges will love this.

* static/script.js
  - What it is: The muscles connecting the bones.
  - What it does: This is the most complex file! It handles 3 major jobs:
    1) It turns on your webcam and draws the dots on your hands using Google MediaPipe.
    2) It takes the translated words (like B + A + T) and stitches them together into full sentences (BAT).
    3) It handles the "Video Call Signaling". When you click "Join Call", this script knocks on the other user's browser and connects the two video streams together over the internet.
    4) It manages the "Text to Speech" buttons and the "Microphone Dictation" in the chat box!

PART 3: IMPRESSIVE TALKING POINTS FOR JUDGES

When a judge walks up to your table, here is how you pitch the technical impressiveness of SignBridge:

1. Lightning Fast Translation (No Cloud Lag)
Instead of sending bulky, heavy LIVE VIDEO frames to a cloud server to figure out what sign language you are doing, we run the vision tracking (Google MediaPipe) DIRECTLY in the user's browser. We only send an incredibly tiny array of 126 numbers across the network to our lightweight Random Forest model. This means translation happens in milliseconds with almost zero latency!

2. Smart Word Construction
Our JavaScript frontend is smart. If the model detects the single letter "B", then "A", then "T", the system automatically stitches them together into the single word "BAT", and the text-to-speech engine speaks the word properly instead of spelling it out.

3. Complete Two-Way Accessibility
We didn't just build a tool for deaf individuals. We built a tool for EVERYONE. 
- Deaf users can sign to the camera, and it generates text and audio for the hearing person.
- Hearing users can click the "Microphone" in the chat box to verbally speak into the app, and the app will instantly dictate their words into text for the deaf person to read. 

4. Peer-to-Peer Video (WebRTC)
Our video streaming is entirely Peer-to-Peer. Once the initial connection is made using our Flask Socket.IO server, the video feeds travel directly between the two users' computers. This keeps server costs at $0 and video quality extremely high.
